Buying a Great Company Is Not Necessarily a Great Investment

In One Sentence

The internet was a genuine industrial revolution and Cisco is still the leader — yet whoever bought it on March 27, 2000 waited 25 years and 8 months to break even. Right about the world, right about the company, right about the revolution; wrong about the price alone, and the outcome is a quarter-century of waiting.

The Core Argument

Buying "the technology" is not buying "the company," and buying "the company" is not buying "the return." Long-run returns have exactly three sources: fundamental growth (belongs to the company), dividends (belong to time), and valuation change (belongs to the moment you click buy). Paying 200× earnings pre-pays a decade-plus of growth and locks the sign of the valuation term negative — every point of growth thereafter first services the optimism you borrowed.

In win-rate and payoff language: a great company at an extreme price may still have a decent win rate, but the payoff is catastrophic; a mediocre company at a deep discount tells an ugly story with beautiful math. Hence one asymmetric sentence:

Great company × terrible price = terrible investment
Ordinary company × discounted price = decent investment

Two Specimens from the Year 2000

Sun Microsystems — fundamentals really can die. The inventor of Java, genuinely profitable (FY2000 net income $2.39bn), peaked near a $200bn market cap at over 10× sales; then Linux plus commodity x86 servers commoditized its proprietary hardware. Sold to Oracle in 2009 for $7.4bn — roughly −96% from the peak. CEO Scott McNealy's famous "10× revenue arithmetic" should hang framed above every tech investor's desk.

Cisco — fundamentals survived, and still couldn't rescue your entry price. Revenue grew 18% even in the crash year; profitable again from FY2002, then two decades of growing revenue, profits, dividends and buybacks — yet the stock took 25 years and 8 months to reclaim its 2000 high, because all of that growth went to digest one day's 200× multiple. Meanwhile a $100/month DCA started in 2002 compounded at roughly 10.5% and stands near six times its cost by July 2026.

Applied to July 2026

SpaceX near 100× sales, Tesla around 350× trailing earnings, Intel priced at triple-digit forward multiples for a yield ramp that has not happened yet — all three may well succeed, but at these prices success is merely the passing grade. Intel happens to have played both sides of the coin: at $17.67 in April 2025 it was the textbook "ordinary company at a discounted price"; five-to-six-fold higher fifteen months later, the same ticker is an entirely different investment.

I do not know which name is this cycle's Cisco and which is its Sun. History does not repeat, but it rhymes — and the rhyming part is never the technology; it is human nature and arithmetic. The market never lacks great companies. It lacks people willing to ask, before buying, "what price am I paying?"
