Key Findings

The Jarque-Bera test gives p = 0.416 — normality cannot be rejected; yet the fitted Student-t has 9.23 degrees of freedom (v<10 counts as significantly fat-tailed), and events beyond 2σ occurred 7 times against 6 predicted by the normal. Fat tails raise the probability of extreme events by roughly 46.7× (a 4σ event: skewed-t 0.2956% vs normal 0.0063%).

Volatility clustering is weak (the ARCH effect is not significant); downside vol / upside vol = 0.92x — the distribution is roughly symmetric with a slight right skew.

The portfolio survives all three scenarios. The key reasons: 19 holdings span multiple sectors, so no single event can destroy every position at once; the defensive buffer of TLT + SGOV + DUK + GIS provides partial hedging in extreme declines; and with no leverage, there is no forced liquidation at the worst prices.

Investment Implications

First, risk should be measured with the Sortino Ratio rather than the Sharpe Ratio — symmetric volatility understates the true risk of concentrated books. Second, VaR should use the Cornish-Fisher correction or a skewed-t fit; normal VaR understates risk by roughly 0.2%. Third, portfolio optimization should replace Mean-Variance with Mean-CVaR: fat tails plus non-zero skew are precisely the worst operating conditions for the Markowitz framework.
